What so special about Bayesian linear models

Bayesian multilevel models

Let \(\alpha\) be the common intercept and \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) be the common coefficients while \(a_j\) is the deviation from the common intercept in the \(j\)-th group and \(\mathbf{b}_j\) is the deviation from the common coefficients. Write the model as: \[y_{ij} = \alpha + \sum_{k = 1}^K \beta_k x_{ik}+\underbrace{a_j + \sum_{k = 1}^K b_{jk} x_{ik}\overbrace{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}^{\mbox{Bayesian error}}}_{\mbox{Frequentist error}}\]

Ben Goodrich, Cambridge 2019

Savage-Dickey density ratio

  • One way of calculating BF for point hypotheses is the Savage-Dickey density ratio (see Wagenmakers et al., 2010).
  • For testing whether a parameter is zero: \[ BF_{10} = \frac{p(D|H_{1})}{p(D|H_{0})} = \frac{p(\theta = 0|D,H_{1})}{p(\theta = 0|D,H_{0})}\]
